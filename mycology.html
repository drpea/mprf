I. Superficial Mycoses
💡
Superficial mycoses = infections limited to the stratum corneum (outermost, dead skin layer). They do NOT invade deeper tissue. Think of them as surface colonizers causing cosmetic changes.

A. Malassezia (Pityriasis Versicolor) HY

Key Features

  • Lipophilic yeast — requires lipid in growth medium
  • Normal skin flora (endogenous cause)
  • 14 species; M. globosa, M. furfur most common
  • Hypo- OR hyperpigmented macules
  • Also causes: dandruff, folliculitis, fungemia in TPN infants

⭐ Diagnostic Clues

  • KOH skin scraping: "Spaghetti and meatballs"
  • Spaghetti = short, unbranched, non-pigmented hyphae
  • Meatballs = spherical yeast cells
  • Skin biopsy: purplish hyphae in stratum corneum
  • Lesions fluoresce under Wood's lamp

Treatment

  • Selenium sulfide (Selsun Blue) — daily application
  • Topical or oral azoles
  • Goal: Reduce to commensal levels, NOT eradicate
B. Tinea Nigra
  • Causative agent: Hortaea (Exophiala) werneckii
  • Dematiaceous = melanin-containing cell walls
  • Asymptomatic, chronic, superficial infection
  • Common in warm coastal areas; young women
  • Clinical: Dark brown to black discoloration on the palm

Diagnosis

  • KOH skin scraping: branched septate hyphae + budding yeast with melanized (brown) cell walls
  • Skin biopsy: brown hyphae in thick stratum corneum

Tx: Keratolytics, salicylic acid, azoles

C. Piedra

Black Piedra

  • Piedraia hortae
  • Hard, nodular infection of hair shaft
  • Tropical countries; scalp hair

White Piedra

  • Trichosporon spp.
  • Large, soft, yellowish nodules on hair
  • Axilla, genitalia, beard, scalp
💊
Treatment for both piedras: Remove infected hair + topical antifungals

🧠
Opportunistic = infects immunocompromised patients. Think AIDS, neutropenia, DKA, transplant. The organism is everywhere — the problem is the host.

A. Candidiasis HY

Morphology

  • Oval budding yeast cells (3–6 µm)
  • Pseudohyphae (buds fail to detach)
  • C. albicans only: forms TRUE hyphae + germ tubes at 37°C
  • Stain: Gram stain, KOH

Clinical Spectrum

  • Thrush: white pseudomembranous lesions, tongue/palate
  • Vulvovaginitis: pruritus, discharge
  • Onychomycosis: painful nail fold swelling
  • Cutaneous: intertriginous (axillae, groin, inframammary folds)
  • Systemic: kidney, skin, eye, heart, meninges

Diagnosis

  • KOH / Gram stain: budding yeast + pseudohyphae
  • Germ tube test: (+) = C. albicans
  • Biopsy: pseudohyphae, vertical orientation (vs horizontal normal flora)
  • Serology: mannan antigen (latex agglutination/EIA); β-(1,3)-d-glucan
⭐
Germ tube test at 37°C → positive for C. albicans. No other Candida species forms germ tubes. This is the classic ID question!
B. Cryptococcosis HY

Key Facts

  • Environmental basidiomycetous yeast
  • C. neoformans: dry pigeon feces, trees, soil (more common)
  • C. gattii: tropical trees (less common)
  • Infection: inhalation → lungs → CNS (meningoencephalitis)
  • Disseminates when immunocompromised
  • Also: skin, eyes, prostate

Morphology

  • Spherical budding yeast (5–10 µm) + thick polysaccharide capsule
  • Capsule = GXM (glucuronoxylomannan) — virulence factor
  • Produces urease
  • Grows at 37°C (unlike nonpathogenic)
  • Produces laccase

Clinical + Dx

  • Chronic meningitis: fever, headache, stiff neck, photophobia, N/V, confusion
  • Elevated CSF pressure, protein, cell count; glucose normal/low
  • India ink (CSF): halo around yeast (capsule outline)
  • Serology: GXM antigen in CSF/serum/urine (latex agglutination, EIA, lateral flow)
  • Culture: growth at 37°C, urease detection
C. Aspergillosis HY

Morphology

  • Septate hyphae, uniform 4 µm width
  • Branch dichotomously at acute angles (~45°)
  • Long conidiophores → terminal vesicle → phialides → chains of conidia
  • Stain: PAS or GMS (hyaline, septate, uniform)

Pathogenesis

Normal: Alveolar macrophages destroy conidia
Immunocompromised: Conidia germinate
Invade preexisting cavities → Aspergilloma
Invade blood vessels → thrombosis/infarction → Invasive

Clinical Spectrum

TypeKey Features
ABPAColonizes bronchial tree; asthma + eosinophilia; Type I + III hypersensitivity; no parenchymal invasion
Aspergilloma ⭐Fungal ball in pre-existing cavity (TB or emphysema); air crescent sign on CT
Invasive ⭐AIDS/neutropenia; hyphae invade blood vessels → necrosis of GIT, kidney, liver, brain
ExtrapulmonarySinuses, ear canal, cornea, nails — localized noninvasive
💊
Aspergilloma: itraconazole, amphotericin B, surgery. Invasive: voriconazole or amphotericin B
D. Mucormycosis / Zygomycosis HY

Key Facts

  • Most common: Rhizopus oryzae
  • Order Mucorales; thermotolerant saprobes
  • Others: Rhizomucor, Lichtheimia, Cunninghamella, Mucor
  • Risk: DKA, lymphoma, immunodeficiency, iron overload, deferoxamine use

⭐ Morphology

  • Broad hyphae (10–15 µm)
  • Uneven thickness; sparse septations (non-septate)
  • Irregular branching (vs Aspergillus's regular acute-angle)
  • Not pigmented

Clinical Findings

  • Rhinocerebral: sporangiospores → nasal → sinuses/eyes/cranial bones/brain; thrombosis, infarction, necrosis
  • Thoracic: lung parenchyma invasion
  • Angioinvasive growth → hemorrhage, thrombosis, necrosis
  • COVID-19-associated (India 2021)

Aspergillus hyphae

  • Septate (many septa)
  • Uniform width (4 µm)
  • Regular, acute angle branching (~45°)
  • Hyaline

Mucor/Rhizopus hyphae

  • Non-septate (sparse)
  • Broad, uneven width (10–15 µm)
  • Irregular, wide-angle branching
  • Not pigmented
E. Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP) HY

Key Facts

  • Causative agent: Pneumocystis jirovecii
  • NOT a protozoan — it's a fungus (Ascomycete)
  • Normal lung flora in healthy people
  • Opportunistic in HIV/AIDS: CD4 < 200
  • Cannot be cultured in lab

⭐ Morphology

  • Thin-walled trophozoites + cysts in tight mass
  • Cysts: thick-walled, spherical (4–6 µm), 4–8 nuclei
  • "Crushed ping-pong balls" on silver stain
  • Also described as: crescent shapes, folded spheres, deflated tennis balls
  • Stains: GMS (silver), toluidine blue, calcofluor white

Clinical + Tx

  • Extracellular pathogen; grows in surfactant layer above alveolar epithelium
  • Dyspnea, nonproductive cough, fever
  • CXR: widespread bilateral infiltrates
  • Tx: TMP-SMX (first-line) or pentamidine isethionate
F. Penicilliosis / Talaromycosis HY
  • Caused by Talaromyces marneffei (formerly Penicillium marneffei)
  • Endemic in Southeast Asia; associated with bamboo rats
  • Risk: HIV/AIDS, TB, steroids, lymphoproliferative diseases
  • Morphology: yeast-like cells (2×6 µm) that divide by fission (NOT budding)
  • Mold form: green-yellow colony with diffusible reddish pigment
  • Clinical: fungemia, skin lesions, reticuloendothelial system involvement
IV. Endemic Mycoses
🌍
Endemic fungi are thermally dimorphic: Mold at 25°C (environment) → Yeast at 37°C (body). All infect via inhalation of conidia → lung → may disseminate. Geographic restriction is a key buzzword trigger!

A. Coccidioidomycosis HY

Pathogenesis Flow

Arthroconidia inhaled (barrel-shaped, in chains)
In host: transform into large spherules
Spherules rupture → release endospores
Endospores form new spherules → infection continues

⭐ Clinical Types

Asymptomatic60%No symptoms
Valley Fever40%Flu-like: fever, malaise, cough, arthralgia, headache; self-limited
Disseminated<1%Immunosuppressed; lung nodules/cavities; skin, bones, meninges

Diagnosis

  • KOH/Culture: examine for spherules
  • Histopathology: H&E, GMS, PAS
  • Serology: Latex agglutination (IgM to coccidioidin), CF, ID tests
  • Coccidioidin skin test: induration ≥5 mm at 24–48 hrs = prior exposure (NOT useful in active disease)
B. Histoplasmosis HY

Key Facts

  • Most common pulmonary fungal infection
  • Causative agent: Histoplasma capsulatum
  • Mold form: hyaline septate hyphae, microconidia (small), macroconidia (large, spherical)
  • MOT: inhalation of conidia from contaminated soil
  • Thermally dimorphic soil saprophyte

⭐ Pathogenesis

Inhale conidia from soil
Conidia → yeast cells; engulfed by alveolar macrophages
Systemic dissemination to reticuloendothelial tissues (liver, spleen, lymph nodes)
Host: granulomatous inflammatory response

Clinical Types

  • Acute Pulmonary: flu-like; hilar lymphadenopathy + pulmonary infiltrates/nodules
  • African: flu-like; less lung, more skin + bone lesions
  • Chronic Pulmonary: reactivation (emphysema)
  • Disseminated: immunosuppressed (elderly, AIDS); lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, fever, anemia

Histopathology + Diagnosis

  • Yeasts within macrophages (facultative intracellular)
  • Stains: GMS, PAS (fungal stains); Giemsa (bone marrow/blood smears)
  • Culture: mold at 25–30°C on SDA/IMA; yeast at 37°C on glucose-cysteine-blood agar
  • Serology: CF tests; histoplasmin skin test (past exposure only)
⭐
The classic image: small yeast cells inside macrophages. If you see Ohio/Mississippi Valley + caves + macrophages with yeasts → Histoplasma!
C. Blastomycosis (North American) HY

Key Facts

  • Causative: Blastomyces dermatitidis
  • Endemic: US + Canada, especially rural riverbanks
  • MOT: spore inhalation OR direct skin inoculation
  • Mold form: hyaline, branching septate hyphae + conidia on slender conidiophores

⭐ Tissue Form (Yeast at 37°C)

  • Thick-walled, spherical yeast (8–15 µm)
  • Single buds with broad base
  • Bud grows to same size as parent
  • This broad-based bud distinguishes Blastomyces from Cryptococcus (narrow-based)

Clinical + Diagnosis

  • Pulmonary infiltrate + lower respiratory symptoms / chronic pneumonia
  • Histology: suppurative + granulomatous inflammation with neutrophils
  • Skin (disseminated): ulcerated verrucous granulomas
  • Microscopy: thick-walled yeast with broad-based buds
  • Serology: CF, ID tests; EIA for Blastomyces antigen A
D. Paracoccidioidomycosis (South American Blastomycosis) HY
  • Causative: Paracoccidioides brasiliensis
  • Thermally dimorphic; South American
  • Mold: chlamydospores + conidia; slow growth
  • Yeast (36°C): large multiply-budding yeast cells = "captain's wheel"
  • Buds attached by narrow connection; larger, thinner walls than B. dermatitidis
  • Patients: 30–60 yr, majority men
  • Initial lesions in lungs
  • Dormancy for decades; then reactivation → chronic pulmonary or dissemination
  • Disseminates: skin, mucocutaneous tissue, lymph nodes, spleen, liver, adrenals
  • Histology: granulomas with central caseation; yeasts in giant cells
  • Serology: most useful for diagnosis; CF or ID test (paracoccidioidin)

Mnemonic Corner

🌡️ "Hot Mold = Cold Yeast"

Dimorphic endemic fungi: Mold in cold environment (25°C), Yeast in warm body (37°C). Rule of thumb: yeast form = pathogenic form in tissue

🍕 Malassezia

Spaghetti + Meatballs = hyphae + yeast cells. Lipophilic = needs fat in media. Treat to reduce, not eradicate.

⚓ Blasto vs Para vs Crypto

Blasto: broad-based bud, 1 bud
Para: multiple buds, "captain's wheel," narrow connections
Crypto: narrow-based bud, with capsule

🎯 Aspergillus vs Mucor

Aspergillus: Septate, Acute angle, Regular → SAR
Mucor: Non-septate, Wide/irregular, Ribbon-like → NWR
Patient with DKA → Mucor, not Aspergillus!

Q1. A 32-year-old HIV-positive patient develops white patches on the tongue and palate. KOH preparation shows oval budding yeast with pseudohyphae. A germ tube test at 37°C is performed. Which finding confirms the most likely causative organism?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. Germ tube formation at 37°C is pathognomonic for Candida albicans. No other Candida species forms germ tubes. This is the classic lab confirmatory test for C. albicans.
Q2. A 45-year-old man with AIDS develops chronic headache, fever, and neck stiffness. CSF examination mixed with India ink shows a halo around budding yeast cells. What is the capsule composed of?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ C. Glucuronoxylomannan (GXM) is the polysaccharide capsule of Cryptococcus neoformans. It is the key virulence factor and the antigen detected in serologic tests (latex agglutination, EIA, lateral flow assay).
Q3. A 54-year-old poorly controlled diabetic presents with facial pain, fever, and a black necrotic lesion on the nasal turbinate. Histopathology reveals broad hyphae (10–15 µm) with sparse septations and irregular branching. What is the MOST COMMON causative agent?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ A. Rhizopus oryzae is the most common cause of mucormycosis. Classic triad: DKA + rhinocerebral involvement + broad non-septate hyphae. Although Mucor is in the name "mucormycosis," Rhizopus oryzae is the leading cause.
Q4. A 29-year-old HIV patient (CD4 = 85) presents with progressive dyspnea and dry cough. Bronchoalveolar lavage stained with GMS reveals round structures resembling "crushed ping-pong balls." What is the first-line treatment?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. TMP-SMX is the first-line treatment for Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia. Pentamidine isethionate is the alternative. The "crushed ping-pong ball" morphology on silver stain + CD4 < 200 = PCP.
Q5. A 38-year-old man returning from exploring a cave in the Ohio River Valley presents with fever, cough, and hilar lymphadenopathy. Bone marrow biopsy reveals small yeast cells inside macrophages. What organism is responsible?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. Histoplasma capsulatum. Geographic clue: Ohio/Mississippi River Valley + bat/bird cave exposure. Key finding: yeast inside macrophages (facultative intracellular pathogen). Dissemination to reticuloendothelial system (liver, spleen, lymph nodes).
Q6. A 23-year-old woman from a tropical coastal area has an asymptomatic black discoloration on her palm. KOH shows branched septate hyphae and budding yeast with melanized cell walls. What unique feature characterizes this organism?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ C. Dematiaceous (melanin-containing) cell walls — this describes Hortaea werneckii, the causative agent of tinea nigra. The melanized cell walls are the hallmark distinguishing feature. Lipid requirement = Malassezia; urease = Cryptococcus.
Q7. A patient with pre-existing pulmonary TB cavities develops hemoptysis. CT shows an "air crescent sign." Sputum culture grows hyphae with conidiophores terminating in a vesicle. What type of hypersensitivity is involved in ABPA?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ D. Type I (immediate) AND Type III (Arthus). ABPA involves both IgE-mediated (Type I) and immune complex-mediated (Type III) hypersensitivity to Aspergillus antigen. Air crescent sign on CT = aspergilloma (fungal ball in TB cavity).
Q8. A 50-year-old man with chronic lung disease and disseminated skin lesions undergoes biopsy. It shows thick-walled yeast cells (8–15 µm) with a single bud attached by a broad base. He lives near a rural Canadian riverbank. What organism?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. Blastomyces dermatitidis. North America (Canada/US, rural rivers) + broad-based single bud + same size as parent yeast + verrucous skin lesions = classic Blastomycosis. Paracoccidioides has MULTIPLE buds (captain's wheel), not a single broad-based one.
Q9. A 22-year-old presents with hypopigmented macules on the upper back. KOH shows "spaghetti and meatballs." What is the PRIMARY treatment GOAL for this condition?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ C. Reduction to commensal levels. Malassezia is normal skin flora — the goal is NOT eradication but to reduce overgrowth to non-pathological levels. Treatment: selenium sulfide (Selsun Blue) or topical/oral azoles.
Q10. Which dermatophyte produces ONLY macroconidia (no microconidia), which are smooth-walled, club-shaped, 2–4 celled, and arranged in small clusters?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ D. Epidermophyton floccosum. This is the ONLY dermatophyte with no microconidia — it produces only smooth-walled, club-shaped, 2–4 celled macroconidia in small clusters. M. canis has echinulate (rough) macroconidia; Trichophyton species have both macro AND microconidia.
Q11. Tinea capitis caused by Microsporum canis vs. Trichophyton tonsurans: which pairing of ectothrix/endothrix and Wood's lamp result is CORRECT?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. M. canis = ECTOTHRIX (spores form sheath AROUND hair shaft) + greenish Wood's lamp fluorescence. T. tonsurans = ENDOTHRIX (spores INSIDE hair shaft) = "black dot tinea" because hairs break at the follicular opening, leaving black dots. Wood's lamp negative for T. tonsurans.
Q12. A 27-year-old travels to Southwestern US and develops fever, arthralgias, cough, and headache after returning. He later develops lung nodules. Sputum shows large thick-walled spherical structures containing endospores. What was the mode of transmission?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ A. Arthroconidia (barrel-shaped spores in chains) are the infectious form of Coccidioides. Once inhaled, they transform into spherules in the host. Arthroconidia are highly infectious. The disease described is Valley Fever (acute pulmonary coccidioidomycosis).
Q13. A 55-year-old man from rural South America presents with chronic cough, mucocutaneous lesions, and granulomatous lymphadenopathy. Microscopy of sputum shows large yeast with multiple buds arranged peripherally around a central cell. The majority of patients are:
Choose the single best answer.
✅ C. Men aged 30–60. Paracoccidioidomycosis (South American blastomycosis) predominantly affects men aged 30–60. The "captain's wheel" morphology (multiple peripheral buds on central yeast cell) is pathognomonic for Paracoccidioides brasiliensis.
Q14. Which organism divides by FISSION (not budding) and produces a green-yellow colony with a diffusible reddish pigment, and is endemic in Southeast Asia?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. Talaromyces marneffei (formerly Penicillium marneffei). Key distinguishing features: yeast cells divide by FISSION (not budding), reddish/red pigment diffuses into culture medium, endemic in SE Asia, associated with bamboo rats and HIV.
Q15. In tinea corporis (ringworm), where should the specimen be collected for highest diagnostic yield?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ C. Periphery (advancing border). Fungal hyphae grow centrifugally (outward from center), making the peripheral advancing border the most active zone with viable fungi. The central area has mostly dead/degenerated hyphae.
Q16. A patient has pruritic vesicular lesions on the sides of her fingers and palm without any evidence of fungi in the lesion itself. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ D. Dermatophytid (id reaction). This is an immune hypersensitivity reaction to a distant fungal infection — NO FUNGI are present in the lesion itself. It is most commonly associated with tinea pedis. Lesions may become secondarily infected with bacteria.
Q17. A neutropenic transplant patient develops fever unresponsive to antibiotics. Serum galactomannan test is positive. CT shows a halo sign. Sputum histology reveals hyaline septate hyphae branching dichotomously at ~45°. The organism is MOST CLOSELY associated with which virulence mechanism in invasive disease?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. Hyphae invading blood vessels causing thrombosis and infarction is the hallmark of invasive aspergillosis. This is angioinvasive behavior → necrotic lesions of GIT, kidney, liver, brain. Galactomannan + neutropenia + 45° branching hyphae = Aspergillus fumigatus.
Q18. Which of the following CORRECTLY pairs the piedra infection with its causative organism?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ A. Black piedra = hard nodules on hair shaft caused by Piedraia hortae. White piedra = large, soft, yellowish nodules caused by Trichosporon spp.. Both infect hair of axilla, genitalia, beard, and scalp. Treatment: remove infected hair + topical antifungals.
Q19. In Histoplasma capsulatum, what term describes its ability to survive and replicate INSIDE macrophages?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ C. Facultative intracellular parasite. H. capsulatum can survive inside macrophages (facultative = can live inside OR outside cells). This allows dissemination to reticuloendothelial system (liver, spleen, lymph nodes). Compare with PCP which is obligate extracellular.
Q20. A positive coccidioidin skin test (induration ≥5 mm at 24–48 hours) in a patient with suspected active coccidioidomycosis indicates which of the following?
Choose the single best answer.
✅ B. Prior exposure, NOT useful for active disease. The coccidioidin skin test only indicates prior exposure (immunity). It is NOT useful for diagnosing active disease. Similarly, the histoplasmin skin test indicates past exposure only. For active disease, use serology (complement fixation, latex agglutination) and culture.

1. A fisherman develops a painful, violaceous raised lesion on his finger after handling fish — NO pus is present. Culture shows a Gram+ bacillus that is H₂S(+) on TSI, catalase(−), α-hemolytic. What is the organism?
AListeria monocytogenes
BErysipelothrix rhusiopathiae
CNocardia brasiliensis
DCorynebacterium diphtheriae
✅ E. rhusiopathiae: ONLY Gram+ rod that produces H₂S on TSI (black butt), α-hemolytic, catalase(−). Causes erysipeloid — zoonotic from fish/pork, NO pus (unlike staph/strep). Treatment: Penicillin G. Resistant to vancomycin.
2. Which drug should NEVER be used to treat Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever?
ADoxycycline
BChloramphenicol
CTrimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole
DTetracycline
✅ Sulfonamides (TMP-SMX) make rickettsial infections WORSE by delaying bacterial clearance. Treatment of choice: Doxycycline. Chloramphenicol is an alternative.
3. A 9-month-old infant presents with hypotonia, constipation, weak cry, and poor feeding ("floppy baby"). Parents mention giving honey. Which is the confirmatory test for this condition?
ABlood culture for C. botulinum
BELISA for toxin in serum
CMouse toxicity (bioassay) test on feces
DPCR for botulinum gene in blood
✅ Mouse bioassay is the GOLD STANDARD for botulism. In INFANTS: specimen is FECES (not serum). In adults/foodborne: serum or leftover food. Mouse dies rapidly = positive. C. botulinum blocks ACh release → flaccid paralysis.
4. A patient on anti-TB therapy develops resistance detected by GeneXpert. Which gene mutation would explain this?
AkatG — resistance to Rifampicin
BrpoB — resistance to Rifampicin
CgyrA — resistance to Isoniazid
DinhA — resistance to Rifampicin
✅ rpoB encodes the β-subunit of RNA polymerase → Rifampicin resistance. GeneXpert ONLY detects rpoB (Rifampicin resistance) — no test for Isoniazid on GeneXpert. katG/inhA = Isoniazid resistance. gyrA = Fluoroquinolone resistance.
5. Ehrlichia chaffeensis characteristically infects which cell type, and what is the diagnostic finding?
AGranulocytes; intranuclear inclusions
BMonocytes; morulae in WBCs (HME)
CLymphocytes; morulae in lymph nodes
DGranulocytes; morulae → HME
✅ E. chaffeensis → Human Monocytic Ehrlichiosis (HME) → monocytes. Anaplasma phagocytophilum → Human Granulocytic Anaplasmosis (HGA) → granulocytes. Diagnosis: morulae in WBCs (most sensitive in 1st week). No rash!
6. A 29-year-old pregnant woman eats unpasteurized cheese, develops fever, and delivers a stillborn with pyogranulomatous lesions. The organism survives refrigeration and shows tumbling motility at 25°C. Treatment?
AAmpicillin
BPenicillin G
CTMP-SMX
DDoxycycline
✅ Listeria monocytogenes: survives refrigerator (4°C), tumbling motility at 22–28°C (NOT 37°C), fecal-oral MOT, causes granulomatosis infantiseptica (early onset <6 days) or neonatal listeriosis (7–28 days). Treatment: Ampicillin.
7. Coxiella burnetii (Q fever) differs from typical Rickettsia in which key way?
AGram-negative coccobacillus with arthropod transmission
BProduces eschar at the bite site
CAcquired by inhalation of aerosols from infected livestock — NOT arthropod bite
DCauses fever and maculopapular rash
✅ Q fever is ATYPICAL — no arthropod vector, no eschar, no rash! Acquired by INHALATION of aerosols from infected animals (cattle, sheep, goats). Prevalent in slaughterhouse workers. Chronic form: infective ENDOCARDITIS (most common). Treatment: Doxycycline. Stains with Gimenez.
8. Which organism causes Lemierre's syndrome (septic thrombophlebitis of the internal jugular vein)?
AFusobacterium nucleatum
BBacteroides fragilis
CPrevotella bivia
DPeptostreptococcus
✅ Fusobacterium nucleatum — thin bacilli with pointed ends; causes Lemierre's syndrome + oral infections + lung abscesses. B. fragilis = most pathogenic non-spore former (intra-abdominal infections, capsule). B. fragilis = only one that grows in bile.

✅ Legionella pneumophila (Legionnaire's Disease). Key clues: hotel/water source exposure, diarrhea, delirium, multilobar CXR, Gram stain negative, BCYE medium (L-cysteine + iron + charcoal). Treatment must have intracellular activity: Macrolides (azithromycin/erythromycin) or Quinolones (levofloxacin). Up to 3 weeks Tx.
10. Which statement about Mycoplasma pneumoniae is TRUE?
AIt is Gram-negative and responds to penicillin
BChest X-ray is always normal in early infection
CEarly infection: mild symptoms but severe CXR consolidation
DHemolytic anemia is caused by warm agglutinins
✅ Paradox of M. pneumoniae ("walking pneumonia"): early infection has mild/moderate symptoms but SEVERE CXR consolidation. No cell wall → NOT Gram+/−, resistant to β-lactams. Hemolytic anemia: COLD agglutinin antibodies (anti-I antigen). Culture: fried egg colonies.
11. A 35-year-old woman develops hand swelling within 10 hours of a cat bite. The isolate is oxidase(+), Gram− coccobacilli, bipolar-staining, penicillin-sensitive. Diagnosis?
AYersinia pestis
BPasteurella multocida
CY. enterocolitica
DE. chaffeensis
✅ Pasteurella multocida: rapid onset (<24h) after cat/dog bite, oxidase+, bipolar Gram− coccobacilli, penicillin-sensitive (DOC: Penicillin G). Yersinia pestis also has bipolar staining but is oxidase−, urease−, and transmitted by fleas/rodents — not cat bites.
12. Which finding differentiates Nocardia from Mycobacterium tuberculosis?
APulmonary infection by inhalation
BPresence of mycolic acids in cell wall
CDissemination to the brain causing abscesses; granulomas/caseation are RARE
DWeakly acid-fast staining
✅ Key differentiator: Nocardia → brain abscesses common; NO granulomas/caseation. TB → granulomas with caseation, Langhans giant cells. Both have mycolic acids (Nocardia has FEWER → weakly AFB). Both cause pulmonary disease by inhalation. Treatment: Nocardia = TMP-SMX; TB = HRZE.
13. The rash of RMSF classically:
AStarts on trunk and spreads outward
BStarts on distal extremities (wrists/ankles) and moves centripetally toward trunk
CBegins on day 1 of illness
DBegins as vesicles on the face
✅ RMSF rash: Appears on day 3–5 (NOT day 1), starts DISTALLY (wrists/ankles/palms/soles) and moves CENTRIPETALLY toward trunk. This centripetal spread from palms and soles is the HIGH-YIELD distinguishing feature. Not all patients have rash.
14. What is the most common form of chronic Q fever?
AAtypical pneumonia (self-resolving)
BCulture-negative endocarditis (especially with valvular disease)
CHepatic granulomas with caseation
DRecurrent vasculitis with rash
✅ Chronic Q fever (>6 months) most commonly presents as INFECTIVE ENDOCARDITIS (culture-negative) especially in patients with pre-existing valvular disease. Diagnosis: Indirect immunofluorescence. Treatment: Doxycycline.
15. A 24-year-old Peruvian man has high fever, pallor, hepatosplenomegaly, and severe hemolytic anemia. Six weeks later, he develops vascular nodular skin lesions but feels well. Which matches his current stage?
AOroya fever — nodular skin lesions, low mortality
BVerruga peruana — vascular skin lesions, low mortality, little systemic reaction
COroya fever — severe anemia, high mortality (85%)
DVerruga peruana — hemorrhage into lymph nodes
✅ B. bacilliformis: Stage 1 (Oroya fever) = severe anemia, hepatosplenomegaly, 85% mortality. Stage 2 (Verruga peruana) = eruptive vascular nodular skin lesions, LOW mortality, minimal systemic reaction. Patient CAN have Stage 2 WITHOUT prior Stage 1.
16. Which Clostridium species is the EXCEPTION — it is NON-motile and CAPSULATED?
AC. tetani
BC. perfringens
CC. botulinum
DC. difficile
✅ C. perfringens = the ONLY Clostridium that is non-motile AND capsulated (most Clostridia are motile with peritrichous flagella). Also unique: subterminal spore (bowling pin shape), aerotolerant, most common cause of gas gangrene (myonecrosis).
17. A patient with HIV has a CD4+ count of 75/μL. They develop fever, night sweats, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. Which organism is most likely?
AMycobacterium tuberculosis
BMycobacterium avium complex (MAC)
CLegionella pneumophila
DNocardia asteroides
✅ MAC: disseminated infection risk when CD4+ <100/μL. Classic symptoms: fever, night sweats, abdominal pain, diarrhea, weight loss. Culture: smooth, non-pigmented colonies. Treatment: Macrolides (clarithromycin/azithromycin) + Ethambutol + Antiretrovirals.
18. Diphtheria toxin causes systemic damage by which mechanism?
AActivates complement → cell lysis
BADP-ribosylation of EF-2 → inhibition of protein synthesis → cell death
CCleaves SNARE proteins → blocks neurotransmitter release
DActivates adenylate cyclase → increased cAMP → edema
✅ Diphtheria toxin (A subunit) catalyzes ADP-ribosylation of EF-2 (elongation factor 2) → inhibits protein synthesis → cell death. This causes myocarditis, neuritis (demyelination), tubular necrosis. Toxin encoded by tox gene (β-prophage). HB-EGF receptor for attachment.
19. A soldier returns from a tropical region with recurrent fever every 5 days, shin pain, and skin papules. He is homeless. Which organism?
ABartonella henselae
BBartonella quintana
CBartonella bacilliformis
DRickettsia typhi
✅ B. quintana (Trench Fever): quintan fever (every 3–5 days, lasts 5 days), shin pain, culture-negative endocarditis, reservoir = humans + body louse, common in homeless. B. henselae = cat scratch disease. B. bacilliformis = Oroya fever (Peru). Treatment: Erythromycin or Doxycycline 2 months.
20. The Nagler reaction is used to identify which organism and which toxin?
AC. tetani — Tetanospasmin
BC. perfringens — α-toxin (lecithinase C)
CC. botulinum — Botulinum toxin
DC. difficile — Toxin B
✅ Nagler reaction = growth on egg yolk agar → dense opalescence caused by α-toxin (lecithinase C) of C. perfringens. Also: stormy fermentation of milk + double/target hemolysis on BAP. α-toxin is the MOST POTENT toxin of C. perfringens → destroys erythrocytes, leukocytes, platelets → hemolysis, necrosis.
21. Which statement about Actinomycosis is CORRECT?
AIt is acid-fast and resembles Mycobacterium
BLesions contain sulfur granules (yellowish ~1mm) in draining sinuses
CGram-negative anaerobic bacillus
DCommonly causes pseudomembranous colitis
✅ Actinomycosis (usually A. israelii): sulfur granules (yellowish, lobulated, ~1mm) in pyogenic draining sinuses. GRAM-POSITIVE filamentous bacilli. NOT acid-fast. 3 types: Cervicofacial ("lumpy jaw" — most common) → Thoracic → Abdominal. Sulfur granules = lobulated tissue cells + bacterial filaments.
22. Plague has a mortality of ~50% untreated. Which type has nearly 100% mortality if untreated?
ABubonic plague
BPneumonic plague
CSepticemic plague
DAll have equal mortality
✅ Pneumonic plague: ~100% mortality untreated. Transmitted by respiratory droplets (person-to-person) → hemorrhagic pneumonia. Most dangerous/infectious form. Treatment: Streptomycin/Gentamicin/Doxycycline/Ciprofloxacin (URGENT). Y. pestis = bipolar "safety-pin" staining on Wright/Giemsa.
23. A Mantoux test in a healthcare worker shows 12mm induration. She received BCG in childhood. IGRA test is positive. Correct interpretation?
AMantoux is false-positive due to BCG; IGRA confirms no TB
BPositive latent TB infection (both tests positive; IGRA not affected by BCG)
CIndeterminate — repeat IGRA needed
DOnly high-risk if HCW with >5mm
✅ HCW = medium risk → positive at >10mm (12mm = positive). IGRA is NOT affected by BCG vaccination (advantage over TST). Both tests positive = latent TB infection. IGRA: TB1 (CD4+) and TB2 (CD4+ + CD8+) >25% of negative control = positive.
24. Which Rickettsialpox feature differentiates it from other spotted fever group?
ATransmitted by tick; produces vasculitis
BNegative Weil-Felix reaction; rash resembles varicella; Papule→Vesicle→Eschar progression
CCauses severe hemorrhagic pneumonia
DOccurs in Far East, 30% mortality
✅ Rickettsialpox (R. akari / mite-borne): NEGATIVE Weil-Felix, varicella-like rash, Papule→Vesicle→Eschar (1 week before fever onset). Milder than RMSF. Agent: R. akari. Vector: Liponyssoides (mite). Transitional group. Treatment: Doxycycline.
25. Bacillary angiomatosis caused by Bartonella shows which histological feature that may be confused with Kaposi sarcoma?
AStellate necrosis surrounded by palisading histiocytes
BLobular capillary proliferation with cuboidal endothelial cells protruding into vascular lumen + epithelioid histiocytes in fibromyxoid stroma
CGranulomas with follicular hyperplasia and microabscesses
DCaseous necrosis with Langhans giant cells
✅ Bacillary angiomatosis (B. quintana > B. henselae): red papule → lobular capillary proliferation + cuboidal endothelial cells + loose fibromyxoid stroma + peliosis hepatis. Resembles Kaposi sarcoma clinically and histologically, but is BACTERIAL (treatable!). Treatment: Erythromycin or Doxycycline 2 months.

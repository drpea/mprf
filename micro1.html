Antibiotic Resistance

MRSA

  • mecA gene → encodes PBP2a (low affinity for β-lactams)
  • Resistant to: all β-lactams (penicillin, methicillin, cephalosporins)
  • Treatment: Vancomycin (± rifampicin)
  • If allergic to vancomycin → Teicoplanin

VRSA

  • vanA + mecA genes
  • Resistant to vancomycin AND methicillin
  • β-lactamase → plasmid controlled
  • MSSA → DOC: Dicloxacillin / Flucloxacillin
S. epidermidis — Prosthetic Device Pathogen
KEY: Low virulence NORMALLY, but forms biofilm on prosthetic devices (artificial valves, catheters, CNS shunts, hip prostheses). Main habitat = skin (100% carriage). Coagulase (−), white pinpoint colonies on BAP, no mannitol fermentation.
Treatment: Vancomycin or nafcillin/exacillin/cephalosporins (often multidrug resistant)

Typhoid Fever Clinical Features

  • Incubation: 10–14 days (range 5–21d)
  • Rose spots — faint salmon-colored maculopapular rash on trunk/chest; resolves 2–5d
  • Prolonged fever (up to 4 wks if untreated)
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Relative bradycardia at peak fever
  • Epistaxis
⭐ Diagnosis: Bone marrow = highest yield culture. Widal test (febrile agglutinins) = simple but low sensitivity. IDL Tubex / Typhidot = more accurate.
⭐ Non-Lactose Fermenter. Culture media: EMB, MacConkey (differential); SS agar, Hektoen (selective); Selenite F broth (enrichment)

Pseudomonas & Other GNB

Non-Fermenters · Oxidase+ · Ubiquitous
Pseudomonas aeruginosa — High Yield Summary

Key Characteristics

  • Ubiquitous gram(−) rod, motile (single polar flagellum)
  • Oxidase POSITIVE (differentiates from Enterobacteriaceae)
  • Non-fermentative (uses oxidative pathway)
  • Grows at 42°C ⭐ (differentiates from other Pseudomonas)
  • Pigments: Pyocyanin (blue) + Pyoverdin (green) → blue-green pus
  • Fruity/grape-like / taco-like / mousy odor
  • Resistant to chemical disinfection; killed by boiling

Virulence Factors

  • Glycocalyx/Alginate → biofilm formation; protects from phagocytes
  • Exotoxin A → inhibits protein synthesis → tissue necrosis (liver target)
  • LPS → endotoxin → fever, shock, DIC, ARDS
  • Pili → host attachment
  • Phospholipase C → invasiveness
⭐ ECTHYMA GANGRENOSUM = hallmark of Pseudomonas sepsis. Erythematous papule → ulcer with hemorrhagic necrosis → dry ulcer (serous fluid only). Surrounded by erythema, no pus.

Cholera — Pathogenesis & Treatment

Pathogenesis:
Mucinase penetrates mucus → attaches to microvilli → Cholera toxin released → ↑cAMP → active secretion H₂O + electrolytes → rice-water stools (up to 20–30L/day!) → profound dehydration → death

#1 cause of mortality = DEHYDRATION (not sepsis — non-invasive!)
Treatment Priority:
1. ORS (Oral Rehydration) — most important!
2. Antibiotics (reduce shedding): Tetracycline (best), Erythromycin/Azithromycin (pregnancy/children), TMP-SMX, doxycycline

El Tor vs Classic:
El Tor = produces hemolysin, Voges-Proskauer ⊕, polymyxin B resistant; 75% asymptomatic
Classic = 50% asymptomatic
Campylobacter jejuni — #1 Cause of Bacterial Gastroenteritis
  • Gram(−), comma/S-shaped/"seagull wing" ⭐
  • Motile: single polar flagellum
  • Catalase ⊕, Oxidase ⊕
  • Hippurate hydrolysis ⊕ = distinguishes C. jejuni from other Campylobacter ⭐
  • Culture: Skirrow's media ⭐; gray to colorless colonies
  • Growth: ↓O₂ (5–7%) + ↑CO₂ (10%) at 42°C

Clinical Features:
Crampy abdominal pain + bloody diarrhea + fever + headache
Invasive (blood + pus in stool)
Self-limiting 5–8 days
Resembles Shigella dysentery
Dark-field microscopy → darting motility
Treatment: Macrolides (erythromycin) ⭐
Helicobacter pylori — Gastric Pathogen
  • Gram(−) spiral-shaped bacilli with lophotrichous flagella
  • Catalase ⊕, Oxidase ⊕
  • Urease ⊕ = DISTINGUISHING CHARACTERISTIC ⭐
  • Urease → NH₃ (alkalinizes gastric acid → survives!)
  • Culture: Skirrow's media; pH 6.0–7.0; gray/translucent colonies
  • Also grows on CAP with antibiotics + horse blood
  • Epidemiology: positive in 40–60% of persons ≥60 years
  • Transmission: oral-oral / fecal-oral (exact route unclear)
Disease Associations:
Antral gastritis → Hypochlorhydria → Peptic ulcer disease → Duodenal/gastric ulceration
Gastric carcinoma (squamous metaplasia) ⭐
Diagnosis Algorithm:
1
Urease test (CLO test) — gastric biopsy; color change; BEST for presumptive dx ⭐
2
Urea breath test — non-invasive; detects urease; can't assess damage extent
3
Stool antigen test; Serum IgG/IgM; Biopsy culture
4
Stains: Giemsa, Warthin-Starry silver stain
🧠 H. pylori Treatment — "Treat My Belly Pain"
Tetracycline (or amoxicillin/clarithromycin) + Metronidazole + Bismuth subsalicylate + PPI (proton-pump inhibitor) → 10–14 days quadruple therapy

Infective Endocarditis — Modified Duke Criteria

Classification

Acute IE: Hectic fever; rapid valve destruction; death within weeks if untreated
Pathogens: S. aureus ⭐, β-strep, pneumococci
Subacute IE: Indolent course; slow progressive damage
Pathogens: Viridans strep ⭐, enterococci, CoNS, HACEK

Duke Criteria — Definite IE

  • 2 Major criteria, OR
  • 1 Major + 3 Minor, OR
  • 5 Minor criteria
  • Possible IE = 1 major + 1 minor; or 3 minor

Major Criteria

  • 2 separate blood cultures with typical IE organisms (persistent bacteremia)
  • Evidence of endocardial involvement on echocardiogram
  • New valvular regurgitation on auscultation

Minor Criteria

  • Predisposition (heart disease / IV drug use)
  • Fever ≥38°C
  • Vascular phenomena (emboli, intracranial hemorrhage)
  • Immunologic phenomena (Osler nodes, GN, rheumatoid factor)
  • Microbiologic evidence not meeting major

    N. meningitidis — Meningococcal Disease

    Key Virulence Factors

    • Capsular polysaccharides — contains NANA (sialic acid) → molecular mimicry → evades phagocytosis
    • Serogroups: A, B, C, X, Y, W-135
    • LPS/Lipid A → vascular necrosis, inflammation, hemorrhagic rashes
    • IgA1 protease → neutralizes host IgA in mucus membranes
    • Pili → attach to nasopharyngeal epithelium
    ⭐ Epidemiology: Occurs in epidemic waves in overcrowded conditions. Sub-Saharan Africa = "meningitis belt" (serogroups A, C). Peak: toddlers 6mo–2yr AND adolescents 10–20yr. Portal = nasopharynx (asymptomatic carriers)

    Clinical Manifestations

    Invasive Meningococcal Disease:
    Flu-like → meningitis + sepsis → COMPLICATIONS:

    Fulminant Meningococcemia:
    High fever + erythematous macular rash → PETECHIAE → large confluent HEMORRHAGIC RASHES (ecchymoses) = HALLMARK ⭐
    Waterhouse-Friderichsen Syndrome:
    Bilateral hemorrhagic necrosis of ADRENAL GLANDS → hypoadrenergic state
    Sequelae: 8th nerve deafness; CNS damage; skin necrosis

    N. gonorrhoeae — Gonorrhea

    Key Virulence

    • Pili → adhere to columnar epithelium (urethra, cervix, conjunctiva, rectum) ⭐
    • LOS (lipooligosaccharide) → endotoxin + molecular mimicry (resembles human glycosphingolipids)
    • Por protein → pore formation; prevents phagosome-lysosome fusion; serum resistance
    • Opa protein → adhesion within colonies; attachment to host cell receptors
    • IgA protease → inactivates secretory IgA
    • Peptidoglycan → activates complement; toxic to fallopian tubes

    Clinical Features by Site

    Urethritis (men)Painful urination; yellow purulent discharge; can be asymptomatic; sequelae → post-gonococcal urethritis (Chlamydia!) ⭐
    Cervicitis (women)Often ASYMPTOMATIC (80%!); mucopurulent vaginal discharge; → PID, ectopic pregnancy, sterility
    Disseminated (DGI)Associated with complement deficiency ⭐; gonococcal dermatitis-arthritis syndrome (skin lesions on hands/feet + arthritis on knees/ankles/wrists); endocarditis; meningitis
    Ophthalmia neonatorumPassage through infected birth canal; mucopurulent conjunctivitis; can → blindness if untreated ⭐; treat with AgNO₃ / erythromycin ointment
    ⭐ Diagnosis: Gram stain (90% sensitive men, 50% women) — gram(−) diplococci WITHIN PMNs. Culture: Modified Thayer-Martin (MTM) agar + 5% CO₂ at 37°C.
    Chlamydia — Obligate Intracellular Parasite
    ⭐ Gram(−) obligate intracellular bacteria with BOTH DNA and RNA. No peptidoglycan layer. Non-motile, non-piliated. Tropism for columnar cells. Cultured in McCoy cells/yolk sac. Culture NOT routinely done.

    Q1. A 3-year-old child presents with high fever and a rapidly spreading petechial rash that has become confluent and hemorrhagic. Which is the most likely diagnosis and causative organism?
    ✓ Correct: C. Petechial eruptions → large confluent hemorrhagic rashes (ecchymoses) is the hallmark of meningococcal disease / fulminant meningococcemia. N. meningitidis causes Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (bilateral adrenal hemorrhage) in severe cases.
    Q2. N. meningitidis can be differentiated from N. gonorrhoeae by which carbohydrate utilization test?
    ✓ Correct: B. N. meningitidis ferments BOTH maltose AND glucose. N. gonorrhoeae ferments GLUCOSE ONLY. Maltose is the differentiating sugar. Memory: N. MeninGitidis = M+G; N. Gonorrhoeae = G only.
    Q3. A 25-year-old woman presents with mucopurulent vaginal discharge. Microscopy shows gram-negative diplococci within PMNs. She is treated for gonorrhea. Two weeks later she returns with milder urethritis. What is the most likely cause?
    ✓ Correct: D. Post-gonococcal urethritis is the frequent sequela of gonorrhea and is caused by Chlamydia trachomatis and Ureaplasma spp. The treatment for gonorrhea does not cover Chlamydia, which has a longer incubation period, so symptoms appear later.
    Q4. A newborn presents with mucopurulent eye discharge 7–12 days after birth. The mother had a history of genital infection. Gram stain shows gram-negative intracellular diplococci. Which treatment is given for prevention?
    ✓ Correct: C. Gonococcal ophthalmia neonatorum prophylaxis = 0.5% erythromycin OR 1% tetracycline OR silver nitrate solution. If untreated, it can rapidly progress to blindness. The 7–12 day timeline is classic for gonorrhea (vs chlamydial conjunctivitis also appears 7–12 days post-birth).
    Q5. A 30-year-old patient has typhoid fever. Which diagnostic test has the HIGHEST yield for culture?
    ✓ Correct: A. Bone marrow culture has the HIGHEST yield for Salmonella typhi. Blood culture is also good but lower yield than bone marrow. Stool culture is useful but variable. The Widal test has limited sensitivity and specificity.
    Q6. A 5-year-old child has a 2-week history of paroxysmal cough with post-tussive vomiting and a characteristic inspiratory "whoop." CBC shows marked lymphocytosis. Which toxin of the causative organism is primarily responsible for the lymphocytosis?
    ✓ Correct: B. Pertussis toxin (TOX) is the PRIMARY virulence factor of B. pertussis. It interferes with lymphocyte trafficking (↑cAMP) → marked lymphocytosis. Also causes histamine sensitization and hypoglycemia. ACT → ↓phagocytosis. DNT → vasoconstriction/necrosis. Tracheal cytotoxin → inhibits cilia.
    Q7. Which of the following Chlamydia trachomatis serotypes causes lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV)?
    ✓ Correct: C. LGV is caused by C. trachomatis serotypes L1, L2, L3 (LGV biovar). A, B, C → trachoma. D–K → inclusion conjunctivitis, urethritis, cervicitis, PID, neonatal conjunctivitis/pneumonia.
    Q8. A 2-year-old presents with bloody diarrhea and acute renal failure. Labs show anemia and thrombocytopenia. Which E. coli pathotype and toxin is responsible?
    ✓ Correct: D. Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS) = anemia + thrombocytopenia + acute renal failure — HALLMARK of EHEC (O157:H7). Caused by Shiga-like toxin (verotoxin) which inhibits protein synthesis (60S ribosomes) → severe cytotoxicity. Grossly bloody diarrhea, little to NO fever.
    Q9. H. influenzae grows as satellite colonies around S. aureus on sheep blood agar (SBA). This is because H. influenzae requires which factors not produced on SBA?
    ✓ Correct: A. Haemophilus requires X factor (hemin) and V factor (NAD). SBA provides the X factor only. S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, and Neisseria spp. produce NAD (V factor) → satellite growth phenomenon (satellitism).
    Q10. A 19-year-old woman presents with UTI. She is sexually active. Culture grows gram-positive cocci in clusters, coagulase-negative, and the organism is resistant to novobiocin. What is the diagnosis?
    ✓ Correct: B. S. saprophyticus = 2nd most common cause of UTI in young sexually active women. Distinguishing features: coagulase (−), novobiocin RESISTANT (unlike S. epidermidis which is sensitive), non-hemolytic, does not contain Protein A.
    Q11. Which of the following is the HALLMARK physical sign of Pseudomonas aeruginosa sepsis?
    ✓ Correct: C. Ecthyma gangrenosum = physical sign/hallmark of Pseudomonas sepsis. Begins as erythematous papule → hemorrhagic necrosis → DRY ulcer (serous fluid only). Surrounded by erythema, usually NO pus. Rose spots = Salmonella (typhoid). Purpura fulminans = meningococcemia. Osler's nodes = endocarditis.
    Q12. V. cholerae colonies on TCBS agar appear yellow because:
    ✓ Correct: D. V. cholerae is a sucrose fermenter. On TCBS agar, sucrose fermentation produces acid → change in pH indicator → YELLOW colonies. Non-sucrose fermenters (V. parahaemolyticus = GREEN; V. vulnificus = BLUE-GREEN).
    Q13. A 40-year-old veterinarian develops progressive weakness, fatigue, and diurnal fever (fever during the day, subsiding at night) after handling animals at a goat farm. Serology shows agglutination titer 1:160. What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment?
    ✓ Correct: B. Brucellosis = "undulant fever" (rising and falling fever pattern). Agglutination titer ≥1:160 is presumptive evidence of recent/current infection. Most commonly from goats (B. melitensis), cattle (B. abortus). Treatment = Doxycycline + Rifampin for 30 days. Children <8y → Gentamicin instead of doxycycline.
    Q14. Which Chlamydia species ONLY contains glycogen in its inclusions?
    ✓ Correct: A. ONLY C. trachomatis contains glycogen in its inclusions (stainable with Lugol's iodine). C. pneumoniae and C. psittaci have NO glycogen. This is the usual distinguisher between C. trachomatis and C. psittaci.
    Q15. Which stain is used to differentiate the elementary body (red) from the host cytoplasm (blue) in Chlamydia?
    ✓ Correct: C. Macchiavello stain: Elementary bodies → RED; host cytoplasm → BLUE. Giemsa stain: Elementary bodies → PURPLE; Reticulate bodies → BLUE. Gram stain is NOT useful for Chlamydia. ZN stain = acid-fast bacteria (Mycobacterium).

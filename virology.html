⚙️ General Properties of Viruses
5.01 · Jan 21, 2026
⭐ Core Concept Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites — they are biologically inert outside host cells. NOT placed in kingdoms. Classified into groups/families.

🧱 Naked vs Enveloped
HARD Mnemonic (Naked viruses)
  • Heat resistant
  • Acid resistant
  • Resist drying
  • Detergent resistant

Enveloped viruses: more fragile, lipid envelope disrupted by detergents/ether. Most appear circular/spherical under EM — except Rhabdovirus (bullet-shaped!)

🔄 Viral Replication Steps
1. Attachment — viral attachment proteins bind host receptors (lock & key)
2. Entry — fusion or endocytosis
3. Uncoating — capsid removed; genome released
4. Transcription — viral mRNA synthesized
5. Replication — genome duplicated
6. Translation — viral proteins made by host ribosomes
7. Assembly — genome + capsid proteins packaged
8. Budding/Release — enveloped: budding acquires lipid envelope; naked: cell lysis
🌀 Reproductive Cycles
Lytic Cycle Replication kills the host cell. Used by virulent viruses.
Lysogenic Cycle Virus replicates without destroying host. Temperate viruses (e.g., lambda phage).
Retrovirus Special:
RNA → (Reverse Transcriptase) → cDNA → dsDNA → integrated as provirus → transcribed by host polymerase

Viral Glycoproteins

  • Hemagglutinin — agglutinates RBCs; facilitates attachment (Orthomyxo, Paramyxo)
  • Neuraminidase (Sialidase) — facilitates release of virions from infected cells
  • Reverse Transcriptase — retroviruses; converts ssRNA → dsDNA

🦠 Influenza: Antigenic Drift vs Shift

Antigenic DRIFT

  • Minor changes
  • Point mutations → AA changes
  • Gradual process
  • Causes seasonal outbreaks
  • → Annual vaccine needed
  • Affects Influenza A, B, C

Antigenic SHIFT

  • Major changes
  • Genetic reassortment of segments
  • Sudden; new subtype appears
  • Causes pandemics
  • Two strains infect same host
  • ONLY Influenza A (animals have related strains)
Influenza Surface Antigens (HA & NA) Vary independently. HA = neutralizing Ab target. NA = sialidase = Oseltamivir target. M2 protein = Amantadine/Rimantadine target.

💊 COVID-19 Treatment Algorithm

Non-hospitalized (high-risk for progression)

1st: Paxlovid (Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir) — oral; within 5 days of symptom onset
2nd: Remdesivir — IV; within 7 days (needs admission)
3rd: Molnupiravir — oral; within 5 days (cheaper; used more in PH)
Risk factors for antiviral indication: Age >60, DM, CKD, COPD, obesity, cancer, serious heart conditions

Hospitalized (requires O₂)

⭐ Dexamethasone = life-saving; given when patient needs oxygen supplementation. Corticosteroid → ↓ inflammation. First evidence-based intervention for hypoxemia.

NOT used (historical/disproven)

Hydroxychloroquine Azithromycin Ivermectin Lopinavir/Ritonavir Oseltamivir Convalescent Plasma Colchicine
Management is purely SUPPORTIVE for all patients. No medicine has been shown to CURE COVID-19.
🏠 Isolation vs Quarantine + Infectious Period

Isolation

  • Separates persons WHO HAVE infectious disease
  • Day 0 = day symptoms appear (symptomatic)
  • Day 0 = day positive test (asymptomatic)

Quarantine

  • Separates persons EXPOSED to infectious disease
  • No need to quarantine if asymptomatic close contact
  • Wear well-fitted mask for 10 days
⭐ Infectious Period: Starts 2.3 days before symptoms; PEAK 0.7 days before onset; infectiousness ↓ from Day 7; lost by Day 10. → Repeat swab NOT needed after Day 7 to label as recovered. RT-PCR can remain (+) up to 6 weeks but does NOT mean active infection.

📈 RT-PCR: CT Value Interpretation
  • Ct (Cycle Threshold) = cycle number where amplification crosses threshold
  • Low Ct = High viral load (earlier amplification)
  • High Ct = Low viral load (later amplification)
  • Successful amplification = sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve
  • Threshold set manually = best practice (set at exponential phase)
Threshold errors:
Too HIGH → false negatives (misses late amplifiers)
Too LOW → false positives
False results:
Non-sigmoidal curve = non-specific amplification = FALSE POSITIVE
Inhibitors → shift curve RIGHT → falsely HIGH Ct

🔄 Enterovirus Pathogenesis Pathway
Portal of Entry: Mouth (enteroviruses) or Nasopharynx (Rhinovirus)
Primary replication: Oropharynx → throat and upper GI
Primary Viremia: Virus enters bloodstream (if no Ab blockage)

💉 HBV Treatment & Chronic Carriers

First-Line Therapy

  • Pegylated interferon alpha-2a
  • Entecavir
  • Tenofovir

Key Facts

  • Neonates: 95% become chronic carriers if infected at birth
  • ⭐ Earlier exposure → higher risk of chronic infection → higher HCC risk
  • Adults infected later: most recover from acute infection
  • Fulminant hepatitis: mostly HBV + HDV co-infection
  • HBV vaccine: given at birth with BCG per national immunization program
HCV Treatment: Pegylated IFN + Ribavirin (standard). Telaprevir/Boceprevir added for genotype 1. NO vaccine for HCV. Anti-HCV stays for LIFE.

💨 Rotavirus Pathogenesis Pathway (HIGH YIELD)
Infects villi of SMALL INTESTINE (spares gastric and colonic mucosa)
Multiplies in cytoplasm of enterocytes; damages transport mechanisms
NSP4 released → Ca²⁺-dependent signal transduction pathway
↑ Ca²⁺ stimulates 5-HT (serotonin) release
5-HT activates 5-HT3 receptors in myenteric plexus → ↑ GI motility
5-HT stimulates VIP release → ↑ cAMP → secretion of NaCl & H₂O → DIARRHEA
5-HT stimulates vagal afferents to vomiting center in brain → VOMITING
Also: Impaired glucose/Na absorption due to damaged villi → replaced by non-absorbing immature crypt cells (restored in 3–8 weeks). Viral excretion lasts 2–12 days (healthy); longer in immunocompromised.

🔁 Antibody-Mediated Enhancement (ADE) — HIGH YIELD
Primary DENV infection → non-neutralizing antibodies produced (low seroprotection)
Secondary infection with DIFFERENT serotype → non-neutralizing Ab ENHANCES viral entry (forms complex via FcR binding; represses antiviral response)
Virus-Ab complex phagocytosed by macrophages → stimulates inflammatory factors → DIC, shock, hemorrhage
⭐ ADE = Major challenge for dengue vaccine development. Vaccine must protect against ALL 4 serotypes. If one serotype not covered → vaccine primes for more severe disease on secondary infection.
Cytokine Storm: TNF-α stimulates DENV-infected endothelial cells → reactive nitrogen/oxygen → Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF) and Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS)

🐕 Rabies — HIGH YIELD
Fatal, vaccine-preventable; affects ALL mammals. No treatment — only supportive. Incubation: 1 week to 1 year (usually 1–3 months).

Virology

  • Family: Rhabdoviridae; Genus: Lyssavirus
  • ss-(−) RNA; enveloped; bullet-shaped (helical nucleocapsid)
  • G glycoprotein = major factor in neuroinvasiveness/pathogenicity
  • Replication in CYTOPLASM

Pathogenesis Pathway ⭐

Virus inoculated (animal bite/exposure)
Replicates in muscle or connective tissue
Binds nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at NMJ + Phosphatidylserine receptors
Travels retrograde along peripheral nerves (retrograde fast axonal transport) → CNS
Replicates in motor neurons of spinal cord + dorsal root ganglia → brain
Brain neurons infected → neuronal dysfunction
Centrifugal spread → salivary glands ⭐, skin, cornea, organs
⭐ Highest titer: Submaxillary salivary gland. Bite at distal foot = longer travel time to CNS than proximal bite. NOT detectable in blood.

🧠 Prion Diseases — KEY POINTS
Prion = Proteinaceous infectious material devoid of nucleic acid. Misfolded PrP(Sc) → induces misfolding of normal PrP(C) → aggregation → neurodegeneration. Fatal; no known remission or recovery.

Disease Hallmarks

  • Disease confined to CNS
  • Spongiform changes (vacuoles = sponge appearance)
  • Neurodegeneration; incubation then progressive chronic disease
  • Fatal; no remission

Types of Human Prion Diseases

DiseaseCause
Sporadic CJD ⭐Most common prion disease; spontaneous transformation; 1/million/yr; age >50
Familial CJDInherited PrP germline mutations; Gerstmann-Sträussler-Scheinker syndrome; Fatal familial insomnia
Iatrogenic CJDIntroduced by physician (contaminated instruments, transplant)
Variant CJD (vCJD)BSE ("Mad Cow Disease"); psychiatric symptoms first, then dementia + ataxia; MRI: pulvinar sign
KuruCannibalism (ritual practices); among Fore people of Papua New Guinea

Prion Resistance / Sensitivity

RESISTANT to: Formaldehyde (3.7%), Urea (8M), Dry heat, Boiling, Ethanol (50%), Proteases, Ionizing radiation
SENSITIVE to: Phenol (90%), Household bleach, Ether, NaOH (2N), 10% SDS, Autoclaving (1 hr, 121°C), Guanidine thiocyanate

Diagnosis of Prion Diseases

  • Clinical history + neurologic exam
  • EEG: periodic sharp waves
  • MRI: cortical ribboning (CJD); pulvinar sign (vCJD)
  • CSF: RT-QuIC (Real-time quaking-induced conversion) — detects prion proteins
  • GOLD STANDARD: Brain biopsy (post-mortem) — vacuolization, NO inflammation
BSE = "Mad Cow Disease" Emerged UK 1986; peaked 1993. Source: infected/contaminated cattle feed. Common agent = Variant CJD. Most recent: "Zombie Deer Disease" (2024) = chronic wasting disease in deer.
🧬 SSPE (Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis)
Slow progressive demyelination in CNS → death. Rare disease of young adults. More prevalent in 3rd world countries (measles endemic). Caused by persistent defective measles virus infection in CNS.
⭐ Diagnostic clue: High titers of anti-measles antibodies BUT LACKING antibodies against M-protein (matrix protein). Young adults. EEG changes. MRI: white matter demyelination.

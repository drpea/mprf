🔑 Key Mnemonics

Plasmodium RBC Preference

P. malariae = Matanda (old) → senescent RBCs
P. vivax/oVale = Vata (young) → reticulocytes
P. falciparum → ALL RBCs regardless of age

Strongyloides vs Hookworm Rhabditiform Larvae

STRONGyloides = STRONGER genitals = LARGER genital primordium + SHORTER buccal cavity
Hookworm = LONG buccal cavity + SMALL genital primordium

Taenia Uterine Branches

T. solium (pork) = <12 branches
T. saginata (beef) = >12 branches

Other Flagellates & Ciliates
Dientamoeba fragilis
  • Flagellate (NOT amoeba despite name)
  • No cyst stage — trophozoite is IS and DS
  • Commonly binucleate
  • Transmitted via Enterobius vermicularis eggs
  • Tx: Iodoquinol
Balantidium coli
  • Only ciliate parasite of humans
  • Reservoir: swine
  • Trophozoite: LARGE (42–120 µm), hairy (cilia), thrown football motility, binucleate (macro + micronucleus)
  • Pathology: ulcer with rounded base, wide neck (vs flask-shaped in E. histo)
Trichomonas vaginalis
  • No cyst form; sexually transmitted
  • Pyriform shape, 4 flagella from single stalk
  • Strawberry cervix / colpitis macularis
  • Tx: Metronidazole (treat both partners)

Protective Factors Against Malaria

G6PD deficiency Sickle cell anemia Thalassemia Hemoglobinopathies

Malarial parasites are less able to interact and multiply in abnormal RBCs → these are the most common Mendelian diseases — driven by malaria selection pressure

Cysticercosis (T. solium)
Life Cycle Algorithm

How humans get cysticercosis

1
Human ingests embryonated eggs (NOT cysticerci) from contaminated food/feces
↓
2
Oncospheres hatch → penetrate intestinal wall → enter bloodstream
↓
3

↓

⭐

Why anemia occurs

  • Neurologic: numbness, paresthesia, loss of vibration sense

1. A patient presents with profuse bloody diarrhea and tenesmus. Biopsy shows flask-shaped ulcers. Which is the most likely organism?
  • A. Balantidium coli
  • B. Giardia duodenalis
  • C. Entamoeba histolytica
  • D. Trichuris trichiura
Flask-shaped ulcers are pathognomonic for E. histolytica amebic colitis. The ulcer has a small entry point through the mucosa expanding into the submucosa. B. coli also causes ulcers but with a rounded base and wide neck.
2. Which Plasmodium species has banana-shaped gametocytes?
  • A. Plasmodium vivax
  • B. Plasmodium falciparum
  • C. Plasmodium malariae
  • D. Plasmodium ovale
P. falciparum is the ONLY Plasmodium species with crescent/banana-shaped gametocytes. All other species (vivax, ovale, malariae) have round or oval gametocytes. This is one of the key identifying morphologic features.
3. A patient is suspected of Enterobius vermicularis infection. What is the most appropriate diagnostic method?
  • A. Scotch tape test in the morning before bathing
  • B. Stool fecalysis for eggs
  • C. Kato-Katz smear
  • D. Baermann funnel sedimentation
The Scotch tape / cellulose tape test done in the morning BEFORE bathing or defecation is the definitive diagnostic method. Enterobius eggs are deposited on perianal folds at night, not usually found in stool. The D-shaped eggs are collected and examined microscopically.
4. Which filarial parasite has a diagnostic stage of UNSHEATHED microfilariae found in skin snips?
  • A. Wuchereria bancrofti
  • B. Brugia malayi
  • C. Onchocerca volvulus
  • D. Loa loa
O. volvulus (river blindness) has UNSHEATHED microfilariae found in SKIN SNIPS because they reside in the skin and subcutaneous tissues. W. bancrofti, B. malayi, and L. loa all have SHEATHED microfilariae found in blood.
5. A 35-year-old from Leyte presents with hematuria and bladder wall thickening. Urine microscopy shows eggs with a terminal spine. What is the causative organism?
  • A. Schistosoma mansoni
  • B. Schistosoma japonicum
  • C. Schistosoma mekongi
  • D. Schistosoma haematobium
Terminal spine + hematuria = S. haematobium (urinary schistosomiasis). The egg's terminal spine is like the bladder leading to the urethra. Note: Leyte has S. japonicum, but the clinical scenario (hematuria + terminal spine) points to S. haematobium regardless of geography stated in the question.
6. Which of the following is TRUE about Toxoplasma gondii?
  • A. Humans are the definitive host capable of producing oocysts
  • B. Oocysts shed in cat feces are unsporulated and must sporulate in 1–5 days to become infective
  • C. Tachyzoites are responsible for tissue cyst formation
  • D. IgM antibodies always indicate recent infection
Cats shed UNSPORULATED oocysts; these need 1–5 days in the environment to sporulate and become infective. Humans are intermediate hosts (NOT definitive). BRADYZOITES (not tachyzoites) form tissue cysts. IgM can persist for up to 18 months, so it does not always mean recent infection.
7. Which is the diagnostic stage of Strongyloides stercoralis that differentiates it from other soil-transmitted helminths?
  • A. Rhabditiform larvae in stool (NOT eggs)
  • B. Embryonated eggs in stool
  • C. Filariform larvae in stool
  • D. Fertilized eggs in stool
Strongyloides stercoralis is unique — its diagnostic stage is RHABDITIFORM LARVAE in stool (not eggs). This is unlike all other STHs where eggs are the diagnostic stage. The rhabditiform larvae have a short buccal cavity and large genital primordium.
8. A patient from Samar presents with portal hypertension and periportal fibrosis on imaging. No cirrhosis. Which organism is most likely responsible?
  • A. Fasciola hepatica
  • B. Clonorchis sinensis
  • C. Schistosoma japonicum
  • D. Echinococcus granulosus
Clay pipestem (Symmers) fibrosis with portal hypertension WITHOUT cirrhosis = classic Schistosoma japonicum (and mansoni) hepatosplenic disease. Samar is endemic for S. japonicum in the Philippines. Eggs trapped in portal venules → granuloma → periportal fibrosis.
9. Which tapeworm can cause megaloblastic anemia by competing for Vitamin B12?
  • A. Taenia solium
  • B. Dibothriocephalus latus
  • C. Echinococcus granulosus
  • D. Taenia saginata
D. latus (fish tapeworm) absorbs Vitamin B12 from the intestine, leading to B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anemia. Clinical signs: neurologic manifestations (numbness, paresthesia, loss of vibration sense), peripheral smear shows megalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils.
10. Which of the following is NOT true regarding Dracunculus medinensis?
  • A. Infective stage is L3 larvae in copepods
  • B. Diagnostic stage is L1 larvae released from the skin ulcer
  • C. Treatment is slow mechanical extraction over several days
  • D. It is a filarial parasite
Dracunculus medinensis is NOT a filarial parasite — this is a major high-yield exam point! It causes Guinea Worm Disease. A, B, and C are all true: IS = L3 in copepods; DS = L1 larvae; treatment = slow mechanical extraction (historically on a twig).
11. A young child living near swine presents with chronic diarrhea, tenesmus, and severe colitis. Stool microscopy shows large ciliated organisms (42–120 µm) with thrown football motility. What is the diagnosis?
  • A. Entamoeba histolytica
  • B. Giardia duodenalis
  • C. Balantidium coli
  • D. Trichuris trichiura
Balantidium coli: large (42–120 µm), hairy (cilia), thrown football motility, binucleate — and the primary reservoir is SWINE. It is the only ciliate parasite of humans. E. histolytica causes flask-shaped ulcers but is much smaller and ameboid.
12. A patient from West Africa develops posterior cervical lymphadenopathy and progressive somnolence. Which organism and its classic sign are most likely?
  • A. T. cruzi — Romaña's sign
  • B. T. b. gambiense — Winterbottom's sign
  • C. T. b. rhodesiense — Winterbottom's sign
  • D. Leishmania donovani — Kala-azar
West African sleeping sickness = T. b. gambiense. Classic sign = Winterbottom's sign (enlarged posterior cervical lymph nodes). It is chronic (death over 1–2 years), unlike rhodesiense which is acute and rapidly fatal. T. cruzi causes Romaña's sign (unilateral eyelid swelling).
13. Which feature most accurately differentiates T. solium from T. saginata?
  • A. T. solium can grow up to 10 meters
  • B. T. saginata has a rostellum with hooks
  • C. T. solium causes cysticercosis in cattle
  • D. T. solium has <12 uterine branches; T. saginata has >12
T. solium (pork) has <12 uterine branches + rostellum with hooks. T. saginata (beef) has >12 uterine branches + NO rostellum/hooks. T. saginata can grow up to 10m. Cysticercosis from T. solium occurs in HUMANS (accidental IH) and pigs — NOT in cattle.
14. Which acid-fast intestinal protozoan is immediately infectious when passed in stool?
  • A. Cryptosporidium parvum
  • B. Cyclospora cayetanensis
  • C. Cystoisospora belli
  • D. All three
Only Cryptosporidium parvum is immediately infectious when passed — because its oocysts are already SPORULATED (with banana-shaped sporozoites) upon excretion. Cyclospora and Cystoisospora are passed as UNSPORULATED oocysts and need days to weeks in the environment to become infective.
15. Which Plasmodium species would cause the longest incubation period?
  • A. P. falciparum (8–11 days)
  • B. P. vivax (10–17 days)
  • C. P. malariae (18–40 days)
  • D. P. ovale (10–17 days)
P. malariae has the LONGEST incubation period of 18–40 days. P. falciparum has the SHORTEST (8–11 days). P. malariae also causes quartan fever (72-hour periodicity), band-form trophozoites, and rosette schizonts — and infects OLDER (senescent) RBCs.
16. Which of the following flukes is correctly matched with its secondary intermediate host?
  • A. Fasciolopsis buski — Freshwater fish
  • B. Paragonimus westermani — Crab/crayfish
  • C. Clonorchis sinensis — Aquatic vegetation
  • D. Fasciola hepatica — Freshwater fish
Paragonimus westermani uses freshwater crab or crayfish as its 2nd intermediate host → eating raw/pickled crab → lung fluke. Fasciolopsis buski → aquatic vegetation (bamboo shoots, water chestnuts). Clonorchis sinensis and Fasciola → also freshwater fish and aquatic vegetation respectively.
17. A 32-year-old returning from Central Africa presents with a firm subcutaneous nodule near his elbow, severe pruritus, and vision problems. Skin snip reveals unsheathed microfilariae. What is the treatment?
  • A. Diethylcarbamazine (DEC)
  • B. Albendazole
  • C. Ivermectin ± nodulectomy
  • D. Mebendazole
This is Onchocerciasis (river blindness) caused by Onchocerca volvulus. Key features: subcutaneous nodules (onchocercoma), pruritic dermatitis, vision problems from dying microfilariae, and UNSHEATHED MF in skin snips. Treatment = Ivermectin (± nodulectomy). DEC is NOT used for onchocerciasis.
18. Which feature differentiates Capillaria philippinensis eggs from Trichuris trichiura eggs?
  • A. Presence of polar plugs
  • B. Barrel-shaped morphology
  • C. Size (35–45 µm vs 50–54 µm)
  • D. Capillaria has a striated shell; Trichuris does not
Both Capillaria philippinensis and Trichuris trichiura have polar plugs and similar shapes. The KEY differentiator is that Capillaria has a STRIATED SHELL while Trichuris does not. This is the pathognomonic feature that distinguishes the two.
19. A 45-year-old woman from southern India presents with nocturnal cough, wheezing, marked eosinophilia, and elevated IgE. Blood smear shows NO microfilariae. What explains the absence of microfilariae?
  • A. Tropical Pulmonary Eosinophilia — microfilariae destroyed in lungs by hypersensitivity reaction
  • B. Microfilariae only circulate during daytime
  • C. Adult worms confined to subcutaneous nodules
  • D. The organism lacks a microfilarial stage
Tropical Pulmonary Eosinophilia (TPE) = "occult" filariasis. Microfilariae are rapidly destroyed in the lungs by a hypersensitivity immune response → AMICROFILAREMIA + marked eosinophilia + high IgE. Common in southern India. Treatment: DEC.
20. Which statement about Echinococcus granulosus is TRUE?
  • A. Definitive host is sheep
  • B. Humans are aberrant dead-end intermediate hosts
  • C. Treatment of choice is Praziquantel
  • D. Infective stage for humans is the protoscolex
E. granulosus: Definitive host = DOGS; Usual intermediate host = sheep; Humans = aberrant dead-end intermediate hosts (ingest embryonated eggs from dog feces → hydatid cysts form). Treatment = SURGICAL RESECTION (not praziquantel). Infective stage for humans = embryonated egg.
